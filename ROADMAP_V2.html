Design document · v1.5 → v2.0

HarvestScape

What the game is right now, the one thing that's wrong with it,
and the four changes that fix it.

The short version. HarvestScape is a finished, polished, genuinely lovely cozy game. It has a real story, a real cast, a real minigame, and a calendar that makes the year turn. It also has one structural flaw that quietly hollows out the whole thing:

Doing nothing with your day is the optimal play.

Part One — What the game is today

A cozy farming game in the shape of Stardew Valley, with RuneScape's 1–99 skill curve. Everything is drawn with code — there isn't a single image file in the project — and every sound is synthesised on the fly.

The world
  • 9 maps — farm, cottage, coop, barn, store, Maya's house, the Guild, a procedurally generated mine, and the coast
  • 12 crops, season- and level-gated
  • 13 recipes, cooked at a stove or campfire
  • Chickens and cows with friendship
  • 4 seasons of 28 days, with weather and seasonal art
The people
  • 6 characters, each with a full heart arc
  • 12 scripted heart events
  • Marriage — to Maya or to Bram
  • A 14-quest story: restore the Guild of Nine Crafts, light the lanterns, then bring Maya's father home
  • 9 torn journal pages, found by doing rather than collecting
The systems
  • A fishing minigame — hold to raise a bar, keep a darting fish inside it
  • 5 skills with mastery milestones at 25 / 50 / 75 / 99
  • A calendar — 4 recurring festivals, 5 birthdays, an anniversary
  • A noticeboard — one villager request each day
  • Rowan's ledger — ~16,000g of restoration projects that change the world
The day
  • Runs 6:00 → 26:00, which is 320 real seconds
  • Energy is 100/day; food is cheap
  • So the real constraint isn't energy — it's time
  • Menus and dialogue pause the clock; fieldwork and fishing don't

Part Two — The diagnosis

I measured an optimal mid-game day, second by second, straight from the code. Here is what it looks like.

One day, played well
TimeWhat you doReal seconds
6:00Water ~50 tiles with the gold can (3×3 coverage — six presses)~40
6:20Harvest & replant whatever is ready (costs zero energy)~0–150
6:40Six hens, four cows. ~690g. Costs no energy.~60
7:00Nothing is required of you for the remaining four minutes.~240

Because crops advance on the newDay() tick regardless of how the day was spent, the highest-value move at 7:00 in the morning is to walk to the bed and sleep. The day collapses to ninety seconds of chores. The other four minutes are a sandbox you are actively rewarded for skipping.

The economy proves it

Sustained profit, per tile, per day: (sell − seed) / days. A 50-tile plot, held for a season:

Starfruit ×50 (passive)3,125g
Pumpkin ×501,500g
Cranberry ×501,100g
Strawberry ×50875g
Turnip ×50375g

Starfruit is a 2× outlier over every other crop. And critically, it costs six energy a day and ninety seconds of attention. Meanwhile:

Fishing, all day (active)4–10k
Mining, all day (active)4–6k
Starfruit (passive, 90s)3,125g

Farming isn't one of three choices. It's a free layer underneath the other two.

The dominant strategy is: keep a starfruit monoculture (passive, ~3,100g), then spend the day fishing or mining on top. Nobody is choosing between them. And the single fastest strategy — scale the plot, sleep immediately — is also the least interesting thing in the game.

What the day actually asks of you

Being brutal, here is every branch point in a day, and whether it's a real decision:

BranchIs it a decision?
Water, or don'tNo — today's weather already decided, and you can't see it coming
Which crop to plantNo — one crop per season dominates by 2×
Fish, mine, or forageBarely — nothing about today makes one better than another
Do the noticeboard requestYes — a genuine, optional, one-per-day goal
Go to bed at 7amNot a decision. It's just correct.

And weather, which should be the answer, does nothing

I grepped every read of state.weather in the codebase. There are three weather types (clear / rain / snow) and exactly one mechanical effect between them:

  • Rain waters your crops for free.
  • Snow does nothing at all — it isn't "rain", so wasRain is false and it never waters. A winter blizzard leaves your frostbloom parched. bug
  • Clear merely permits fireflies to spawn.
  • Fishing, foraging, the mine and the beach never read the weather at all.
  • There is no forecast. You learn today's weather by waking up in it.

One more, found while measuring: upgrading the Hoe does literally nothing. The Hoe branch of useTool() never reads power or a coverage radius — it tills one tile for two energy at Basic, Copper, Iron and Gold alike. You can spend 6,500g and five Gold Ore on an upgrade with zero effect. bug

Part Three — What I got wrong

My first instinct was to add five features: a forecast, a fishing hunt, an orchard, a mine overhaul, and a Guild endgame. I sent that plan to four independent critics — a game designer, a cozy-genre player, an engineer who'd have to build it, and a loop analyst — and asked them to attack it.

They converged, unprompted, on the same sentence:

"The proposal adds things to do with the afternoon, but never removes the reason to skip the afternoon."

They were right. Every one of those five features was flavour poured on top of a broken incentive. Ship all five and the optimizer still tends the starfruit and sleeps at seven. So the plan changed. Two features were cut, one was rewritten, and the actual fix — which wasn't in the original plan at all — moved to the front.

Cut cut

The Guild Blessings. "Each dawn, walk to the Guild and pick a buff." It cannibalised the fishing hunt (a blessing that relaxes a legendary's conditions deletes the thing you just built), and it re-introduced the exact disease — a mandatory ninety-second dawn chore.

Cut cut

The Deep (mine overhaul). The most engineering for the least effect on the actual problem. And "you may not descend without the lantern" is a gate, and a gate is friction. In a game about being cared for, the world should not wag its finger at you.

Part Four — v2.0 · "A Day Worth Staying Up For"

Thesis. Make the day itself worth living. Every day should offer something that exists only today; you should be able to see it coming; and no single crop should quietly pay for everything so that none of it matters.

Rule. Nothing is ever taken from the player. No crop is destroyed, no day is stolen, no gate is locked. The world gives more on some days than others — and tells you in advance which days those are.

1 · Tom's Demand — the market remembers newS

This is the fix. It goes first because nothing else matters until it lands.

Sell many of the same thing in one day and its price slides: the first sells at full price, the tenth at ~70%, the fortieth at the floor. It resets every morning. Tom will tell you plainly: "I can't shift forty starfruit, farmer. Bring me something else."

  • Why: a fifty-tile starfruit monoculture drops from ~3,100g/day to roughly ~1,400g. Not by nerfing starfruit — by nerfing sameness. Diversity pays. The noticeboard, cooking, foraging, fishing and the orchard all become economically real overnight.
  • Cozy: nothing is lost. You simply get less for the fortieth identical thing, and the shop shows you the price before you sell.
  • Chore check: ignorable entirely. If you never sell in bulk you never see it.

2 · The Forecast & the Day's Offering newM

Five weathers instead of three: clear · rain · storm · fog · snow. Tomorrow's weather is rolled tonight and posted on the noticeboard, shown in the HUD, and logged in the Almanac. Each weather makes the world offer something — today only:

WeatherWhat the valley offers
ClearFireflies. A good day for a festival, or for a wedding.
RainThe fish bite fast. Forage gives double. Your crops water themselves.
StormThe sea is too rough to fish — but the veins run rich underground. And the next morning the beach is strewn with storm-wrack: pearls, coral, lost tackle.
FogThe deep seams read richer. Gems come easier. The valley is very quiet.
SnowFrostberries in the meadow. The cold firms the flesh — winter fish fetch a premium.
  • Why it defeats the sleep-skip: storm-wrack, doubled forage and the legendary bite-windows exist only that day. Sleeping through them loses them. No punishment — just a thing you'd rather not miss.
  • The storm is never "closed." Bram says the sea's too rough today. Nothing is taken. The next morning, the sea gives it back.
  • Festivals and birthdays force fair weather. The coast never closes on a dated event.
  • Chore check: you may ignore the forecast forever and play exactly as you do today.

3 · The Hunt — fishing gets places, hours and legends newM

Right now every body of water in the valley contains the same fish. In v2.0 the catch depends on where you stand, what hour it is, what the weather's doing, and what season it is.

And there are five legendary fish, one for each season and one that only rises in a storm. Each has exact conditions. You don't find them in a wiki — Bram tells you, one clue per heart, and every clue you learn writes itself into your Almanac as a checklist.

  • Why: it converts a shallow slot-machine into a hunt, using the minigame that already exists. It's the biggest consumer of the forecast — the reason to care that tomorrow is foggy.
  • Cozy: a secret whispered by a friend, not homework. Nothing is missable — the seasons come round again.
  • Chore check: entirely optional. The ordinary fish are still there.

4 · The Orchard & the Apiary newM

Buy a sapling. Plant it anywhere on the farm. It takes a whole season to mature — and then it fruits every day of its season, forever. A cherry tree is a promise you make to your future self.

Beehives produce honey, and the yield scales with how many flowering tiles lie within four squares of the hive. Flowers can't be sold. So every flower tile is opportunity cost against a crop tile.

  • Why: it's the only feature that changes what your farm is, rather than what you do on a given afternoon. It gives the shape of the land a meaning, and it gives day 100 something to build toward.
  • Balance: fruit yields sit deliberately under starfruit per tile. Trees are diverse income, which — thanks to Tom's Demand — is now worth more than a bigger monoculture.
  • Chore check: a tree you never harvest simply keeps its fruit. Nothing rots, nothing nags.

Two bugs, fixed on the way fixS

  • The Hoe upgrade will do something. Copper tills a 3-tile row, Iron a 5-tile row, Gold a 3×3 — matching the watering can, for one energy each. 6,500g should buy you something.
  • Snow will be honest. Snow does not water your crops — the ground is frozen — and the Almanac will say so plainly, so winter reads as a deliberate constraint rather than a bug.

Part Five — Why these four belong together

Tom's Demand stops one crop from paying for everything, which pushes you out of the farm and into the day.

The Forecast gives that day a character, and tells you its character in advance, so leaving the farm is a plan rather than a wander.

The Hunt is what the forecast is for — the biggest, most specific reason to be somewhere particular, at a particular hour, in particular weather.

The Orchard is what you build with the gold you earn out there — and because it pays in variety (apples, cherries, plums, honey), Tom's Demand rewards it more than another row of starfruit ever could.

Remove any one and the others weaken. Cut Tom's Demand and the optimizer still sleeps at seven. Cut the forecast and the Hunt becomes a lottery. Cut the Hunt and the forecast is a decoration. Cut the Orchard and there's nothing to spend the day's earnings on.

And once in a while — by design — two good things happen on the same day and you can't have both. Fog means the gem seams are rich, and it's also the only weather the Whitefin rises in. That single collision is the cozy-safe version of a hard choice: nothing is lost either way, but you'll think about it.

Build order

  1. Tom's Demand — the rebalance lands first, because every later feature is measured against it.
  2. The two bug fixes — the Hoe tiers and the honest snow. Cheap, and immediately felt.
  3. The Forecast & the Day's Offering — the spine. Five weathers, tomorrow posted tonight, present-only rewards.
  4. The Hunt — fish tables by place, hour, weather and season; five legends; Bram's clues; the Almanac checklist.
  5. The Orchard & the Apiary — saplings, seasons, honey, and a farm whose shape means something.
  6. Adversarial review & regression — the same discipline as every release: independent reviewers, three-lens verification, and a full pass over every map, every save version, every festival date.

Explicitly not doing

  • Combat. The game ships zero combat and that is an identity, not an oversight.
  • Crop destruction, lightning, dead animals. Nothing is ever taken from the player.
  • The Guild blessing board. A dawn chore that eats the Hunt.
  • The mine overhaul. Most engineering, least effect on the real problem. Weather gives the mine its good days instead. Maybe v2.1.
  • NPC schedules, quality tiers, sprinklers, a greenhouse, children. All previously considered, all previously cut, all still cut.

Status — shipped

All four features plus the two bug fixes are built, reviewed and live in v2.0. Verified numbers from the finished code:

  • Tom's Demand: bulk-dumping 50 starfruit now nets ~1,100g/day (was 3,125); a staggered daily farmer loses ~4%. The sleep-at-7am optimum is gone.
  • The Forecast: five weathers, tomorrow chalked on the board tonight, present-only offerings. Zero storms or fog on any festival or birthday.
  • The Hunt: five legends, each provably catchable (fishDiff hard-clamped so no fish can out-swim the bar); pond and coast hold different fish; Bram drips one clue per heart.
  • The Orchard & Apiary: trees pay 17–25 g/tile/day averaged over a year vs starfruit's 63; four hives cap at 1,200g/day and honey obeys demand like everything else.

An adversarial review (six dimensions, three-lens verification) confirmed two real defects — a permanent tree could wall off the cottage door, and a lightning flash could freeze behind a menu. Both fixed; trees and hives can now be dug up, so no placement is ever permanent. Full regression green: 9 maps × 5 weathers × 6 dates, 288 mine floors reachable, Act Two intact, v1.5 saves migrate.
