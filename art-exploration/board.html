Faces worth remembering

Four ways to give HarvestScape's people a memorable face — in the world and in conversation — each shown on the same three characters so the only thing changing is the style.

The brief, in your words: the character sprites are too pixelated, there aren't enough defining features, and nobody's memorable — not even the main character. The conversation portraits should be higher-fidelity and more distinct — cozy, unique, not too anime, and not a Stardew clone.

Where we are now — the live build

Every dialogue portrait is the same 64×64 head template recolored; every world body is the same 16×22 rig recolored. Hair colour aside, the faces don't individuate — which is exactly why nobody sticks in memory. The problem isn't only resolution; it's that there's one silhouette wearing seven palettes.

The constraint that shapes every option

HarvestScape is 100% procedural — so this is really two problems

There are no image files in the game; every sprite is drawn in canvas code. That single rule splits "better characters" into two very different jobs, and they don't have the same answer:

World sprites

16×22, drawn every frame, in many poses and directions. These must stay pixel and procedural to fit the real-time canvas pipeline. The fix here is more resolution and a distinct silhouette per person — not painting.

Dialogue portraits

Shown large, one at a time, static. This is where real illustration fidelity is feasible and pays off most — and where an anime-vs-storybook-vs-woodcut decision actually lives.

So the honest shape of the answer is a hybrid: a pixel world with illustrated faces — which, not coincidentally, is exactly how Stardew Valley itself is built. The four directions below are really four options for the faces, each paired with the sprite treatment that matches it.

Before style — the memorability fix

"Memorable" is a design decision before it's a resolution one

A character sticks when the eye can catch one thing in a single frame — a silhouette, a prop, a colour. Give each person a signature and they read at 16 pixels or 1600. These three signatures carry across all four styles below — it's why you recognise the same people every time.

Four directions

The same valley, four ways

For each: the three anchor portraits, a world-sprite reference, how it would actually ship in the engine, and where it sits on the two lines you flagged — anime-ness and Stardew-likeness — plus build effort and how well it fits the no-assets rule.

If I'm picking

A recommendation, and the one real decision

World sprites → Harvest HD. Faces → Lamplight (painterly). — the Stardew-proven combo: a crisp pixel world with warm illustrated faces, and the furthest of any option from both "anime" and "Stardew clone."

Harvest HD is the only face-style that also answers the world problem for free — it's pixel, so it stays procedural and fixes the "same rig recolored" sprites at the same time. Lay warm painted portraits on top and conversations get the fidelity jump you're after. Meadowfolk is the one I'd steer away from given your "not too anime" note — it's the closest of the four to that line. Woodcut Folk is the pick if you'd rather HarvestScape look like nothing else on the shelf — it's the boldest identity, at the cost of being the biggest departure from the current cozy-realistic tone.

The decision underneath it

Everything except Harvest HD means baking a small set of portrait image files — a real, contained break from the "100% procedural, no assets" rule in CLAUDE.md. That's a values call only you can make. Three honest paths:

Path A · purest
Stay 100% procedural

Harvest HD everywhere — hand-code higher-res pixel portraits + sprites. No rule broken. Ceiling is "gorgeous pixel," not "painted."

Path B · recommended
Bake a portrait set

Keep the pixel world procedural; ship a curated folder of ~7 characters × a few expressions as baked art (Lamplight or Woodcut). Biggest payoff, contained scope.

Path C · concept-only
North-star, then reproduce

Treat these images as targets and hand-reproduce the closest look in-engine. Keeps the rule; slower, and the painterly styles can't fully survive the trip.

If you like a direction, next steps

  1. Pick a face style + a path (A/B/C above). That's the only blocking decision.
  2. Lock the protagonist first. The hero's face is the one you said you can't remember — nail one canonical look and expression sheet before anyone else.
  3. Prove one conversation end-to-end in the real dialogue box at game scale — portrait + name + text — before committing to all seven.
  4. Roll the Harvest HD silhouette pass into the world sprites regardless of the face choice — distinct hats, builds and props are the cheapest memorability win and need no new assets.
